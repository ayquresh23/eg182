General Manufacturing

What is manufacturing?

Forget the textbook definition for a second. Manufacturing is just turning stuff into other stuff. You take raw materials — metal ore from a mine, oil from the ground, sand from a beach — and through a series of processes, transform them into things people want to buy. A car. A phone. A pen.

The module gives manufacturing two formal definitions that you need to know:

Technological definition

Manufacturing is the application of physical and chemical processes to change the geometry, properties, or appearance of a starting material to make parts or products.

Economic definition

Manufacturing is the transformation of raw materials into items of greater value. Each manufacturing step adds value — that's the whole point of it economically.

🪵
Think of a tree. As raw timber it's worth £50. Cut into planks — £150. Made into a chair — £500. Sold in a furniture shop with a brand — £2000. That's value being added at each manufacturing step.

Operations: processing vs assembly

Every manufacturing operation falls into one of two categories. Understanding the difference matters because they require completely different equipment, skills, and considerations.

Processing operations

Transform a single material — change its shape, properties, or appearance.

  • Casting — pouring molten metal into a mould
  • Machining — removing material with a cutting tool
  • Moulding — pressing plastic into a die
  • Forging — hammering metal into shape
  • Heat treatment — changing properties through heating/cooling
Assembly operations

Join two or more components together — no transformation, just connection.

  • Welding — fusing metals together
  • Bolting — mechanical fasteners
  • Adhesive bonding — glues
  • Brazing & soldering — joining with filler metals
  • Riveting — permanent mechanical joins

Consumer vs capital goods

Not all products are sold to the same people. The module splits manufactured goods into two categories based on who buys them.

Consumer goods

Bought by individuals for personal use.

Cars Phones Tyres Clothes Tennis rackets
Capital goods

Bought by companies to make other goods.

Lathes Aircraft Robots Industrial ovens CNC machines

An aircraft is a capital good when British Airways buys it to fly passengers. The same aircraft becomes a consumer good if a wealthy individual bought it for private use. Who buys it determines the category.

Industry structure — three tiers

All economic activity sits in one of three industry tiers. Manufacturing is firmly in the middle one, which is why this module is called Manufacturing Technology.

Primary industry

Extracts raw materials from nature.

Agriculture Fishing Mining Forestry Oil drilling

Secondary industry

This module. Transforms primary materials into products. Manufacturing.

Steel mills Car factories Plastics plants Chemical plants

Tertiary industry

Services — no physical product is made.

Restaurants Banking Retail Healthcare Education

Production quantity & variety

A factory making 10 custom yachts a year operates completely differently from one making 10 million identical bottle caps. The relationship between quantity and variety is fundamental to choosing manufacturing methods.

Key insight

Quantity and variety trade off against each other. You can make many different things in low quantities, OR a few things in high quantities. Trying to do both gets expensive fast — that's why factories specialise.

Net shape & near-net shape

Two terms you'll hear repeatedly across the module — they describe how close a manufacturing process gets you to the final part dimensions:

Net shape

Part comes out of the process at final dimensions. No further machining needed. Most efficient — no wasted material.

Example: precision die casting, MIM, some additive manufacturing.

Near-net shape

Part requires only minimal finishing — typically light machining or grinding to hit final tolerances.

Example: most powder metallurgy, investment casting, drop forging.

The basics

Imagine you want to make a metal teapot. You could machine it from a solid block of aluminium — slow and expensive. You could forge it from sheet metal — works for simple shapes but not curves. Or you could cast it — make a teapot-shaped hollow in some material, pour molten metal in, let it cool, break out the part.

Casting is one of the oldest manufacturing processes — bronze swords were cast 5,000 years ago. The principle hasn't changed:

Finish

Why use casting? Three big advantages:

  • Complex shapes — internal cavities, curved surfaces, hollow features
  • Large parts — engine blocks, ship propellers, statues
  • Variety of metals — almost any metal that can be melted can be cast

Mould types — expendable vs permanent

The mould is half the manufacturing process. Two fundamental categories:

Expendable moulds

Destroyed after each casting. New mould per part.

  • Sand casting (most common)
  • Investment casting (lost wax)
  • Lost foam (EPS pattern)

Pros: complex shapes, any metal, low tooling cost.
Cons: slower, single-use, more material handling.

Permanent moulds

Reused thousands of times. Made of metal.

  • Die casting (most common)
  • Centrifugal casting
  • Slush casting

Pros: fast, consistent, smooth finish.
Cons: limited to lower melting metals, expensive tooling, restricted to high volumes.

Sand casting anatomy

Sand casting is the most common and oldest casting method. The mould is made by packing sand around a wooden or metal pattern, then removing the pattern to leave a cavity. Molten metal fills the cavity, solidifies, and the sand is broken away.

Every component of a sand mould has a specific job. Learn the anatomy because the exam tests it directly.

Riser

Why is the sprue tapered?

As metal flows down by gravity, it accelerates. If the sprue had constant cross-section, the falling metal would create suction and pull air in — causing porosity. The taper keeps the metal column full at all times.

Sand properties — the five (plus extras)

You can't just use any sand. Foundry sand needs five specific properties — these are direct exam MCQ targets:

1. Strength

Holds shape and resists erosion when hot metal is poured in.

2. Permeability

Allows hot gases to escape through voids between sand grains. Without this, gas gets trapped → porosity.

3. Thermal stability

Resists cracking on contact with molten metal. Silica (SiO₂) is the main material.

4. Collapsibility

Gives way as the casting shrinks during cooling. If sand was too rigid, casting would crack (hot tearing).

5. Reusability

Sand from broken moulds can be recycled into new moulds, reducing cost.

+ Refractory

Withstands high temperatures without degrading — this is why silica works.

Standard sand mix

90% sand (silica) + 7% clay (binder) + 3% water (activator)

Small grain = better surface finish. Large grain = better permeability. Always a trade-off.

Solidification theory

When you pour molten metal into a mould, it doesn't just freeze in place. The way it solidifies determines the final grain structure and therefore the mechanical properties of the casting. This section is heavy on physics — but it explains why castings behave the way they do.

Pure metals vs alloys — different behaviour

Pure metals

Freeze at one specific temperature. The cooling curve has a flat section called thermal arrest — temperature stays constant while latent heat of fusion is released.

Alloys

Freeze over a temperature range (between liquidus and solidus). Within this range — the mushy zone — the metal is part liquid, part solid.

Three solidification zones

As metal solidifies in a mould, three distinct grain structures form from outside to inside:

1. Chill zone

Outermost layer — touches cold mould wall, freezes instantly. Very fine, randomly oriented grains.

2. Columnar zone

Middle layer — grains grow inward against heat flow direction. Long, parallel, anisotropic (different properties in different directions).

3. Equiaxed zone

Centre — equal-sized grains in all directions. Isotropic — best mechanical properties for general use.

Three grain structure zones — symmetric cross-section of solidifying metal casting

Three shrinkage stages

Metal contracts in three distinct stages as it cools from molten to room temperature:

  1. Liquid contraction — molten metal volume reduces as temperature drops
  2. Solidification shrinkage — biggest stage. Metal contracts as it changes from liquid to solid. This is what the riser feeds.
  3. Solid thermal contraction — solid metal continues to shrink as it cools to room temperature
⚠️ Why risers exist

Solidification shrinkage causes voids in the casting if there's no extra metal to feed them. The riser is a reservoir of molten metal that solidifies LAST — feeding liquid metal into the shrinking cavity. Riser must have a higher V/A ratio than the casting to ensure it solidifies last (Chvorinov's rule).

Casting defects

Things that go wrong. Each defect has specific causes — exam MCQs often test what causes what.

Porosity

Voids from trapped air or solidification shrinkage. Weakens the part. Caused by turbulent pouring or poor riser design.

Cold shut

Two flow fronts meet but don't fuse — premature freezing creates a visible seam and stress concentration.

Misrun

Metal solidifies before completely filling cavity — either too cold, too slow, or section too thin.

Cold shots

Metal splatters during pouring — solid globules become entrapped in casting.

Hot tearing

Cracking caused by RIGID mould restricting thermal contraction during cooling. ⚠️ Common exam trap — NOT caused by flexible mould.

Sand blow / wash

Blow = balloon-shaped gas cavity. Wash = sand erosion creating embedded particles. Both from gas/erosion during pouring.

Why "lost wax"?

The wax is melted away (lost) before the metal is poured. But the wax can be recovered and reused — that's an MCQ trap. Just because it's "lost" from the mould doesn't mean it's discarded.

Die casting

Permanent metal mould (the die) with molten metal injected under high pressure. Two variants:

Hot chamber

Injection mechanism is submerged in the melt. Fast, continuous cycle.

Limited to low-melting metals — zinc, tin alloys. Reactive metals would corrode the mechanism.

Cold chamber

Injection mechanism is separate from the melt. Metal ladled in for each shot.

Used for reactive/high-temperature metals — aluminium, magnesium, copper.

Centrifugal casting

Mould spins at high speed during pouring. Centrifugal force flings metal outward against the mould wall — hollow centre forms naturally, no core needed. Limited to axisymmetric shapes (pipes, tubes, rings, train wheels).

Worked example — Chvorinov's rule

A cube casting (40mm side) needs a cylindrical riser. Mould constant C = 4 s/mm². If the riser must solidify 25% slower than the casting, find the dimensions of a riser with H/D ratio = 1.

Step 1: Calculate V/A for the cube. V = 40³ = 64,000 mm³. A = 6 × 40² = 9,600 mm². V/A = 6.67 mm.
Step 2: Casting Ts = 4 × (6.67)² = 178 s.
Step 3: Riser must take 25% longer → Tr = 178 × 1.25 = 222 s.
Step 4: For riser, (V/A)² = 222 / 4 = 55.6 → V/A = 7.45 mm.
Step 5: For cylinder with H = D: V = π(D/2)²×D = πD³/4. A = 2π(D/2)² + πD×D = πD²/2 + πD² = 3πD²/2.
Step 6: V/A = (πD³/4) / (3πD²/2) = D/6. So D/6 = 7.45 → D = 44.7 mm, H = 44.7 mm.
Answer: Riser diameter and height = 44.7 mm

Topic 2 — exam traps

Trap 1
"Hot tearing is caused by flexible moulds restricting shrinkage."
FALSE. Hot tearing is caused by RIGID moulds restricting thermal contraction during cooling.
Trap 2
"In investment casting, the wax pattern is destroyed and cannot be recovered."
FALSE. Wax can be recovered and reused — it's melted out, but it doesn't disappear. The pattern is "lost" from the mould but the wax itself is recyclable.
Trap 3
"Investment casting can produce very large parts."
FALSE. Investment casting IS size-limited. The ceramic shell becomes too heavy and fragile for large parts. Used for small, complex, high-precision components.
Trap 4
"Lost foam casting cannot have core shift defects because there's no core."
FALSE. Lost foam can have core shift defects when foam patterns deform during pouring. No conventional core ≠ no core-related defects.
Trap 5
"Centrifugal casting can produce any shape since the mould spins."
FALSE. Centrifugal casting only produces axisymmetric shapes — pipes, tubes, rings, wheels. Anything that's symmetrical about a rotation axis.

Two fundamental types — TP vs TS

Before we touch any process, you need to understand the two categories of plastic. They behave completely differently and dictate which processes you can use.

Thermoplastics (TP)

Re-meltable, recyclable. Heat them, they soften. Cool them, they solidify. Repeat as many times as you like.

Examples: PE, PP, PVC, PET, PS, ABS, nylon

🧈
Like butter. Melt it, set it, melt it again.
Thermosets (TS)

Permanently cured. Heat triggers a chemical reaction (curing) that permanently cross-links the molecules. Once cured, can never be remelted.

Examples: epoxy, polyester resin, phenolic, melamine, vulcanised rubber

🧱
Like concrete. Once it sets, it's set forever.
Why this matters

Some processes require the plastic to be re-melted from a solid form (like thermoforming, which heats a solid sheet). These processes only work with TP. Other processes use the curing reaction itself (like compression moulding) and only work with TS.

Polymer melt properties — three key concepts

When you melt a plastic, it behaves differently from a normal liquid like water. Three properties make plastic processing what it is.

1. Viscosity — resistance to flow

Honey is more viscous than water. For polymers, viscosity depends on:

  • Temperature — higher temp = lower viscosity = easier flow
  • Molecular weight — longer chains = higher viscosity
  • Shear rate — faster pushing = lower viscosity (shear thinning)

2. Shear thinning — non-Newtonian behaviour

This is the weird one. Water has the same viscosity whether you push it slowly or quickly. Polymer melts get thinner the faster you push them.

🍅
Like ketchup. Shake the bottle slowly — nothing comes out. Shake it hard and fast — it suddenly flows. Same physics in polymer extrusion: pushing harder makes it flow easier.

This behaviour is called pseudoplastic or shear thinning. It's why high-pressure injection moulding works — the polymer flows easily into complex moulds under high pressure.

3. Viscoelasticity — elastic memory

Polymer chains have memory. When forced through a die, they stretch and align. When they exit the die and the pressure releases, they want to return to their natural coiled state.

Die swell

The polymer extrudate swells larger than the die opening as it exits — chains relaxing back to their coiled state. Dies must be designed smaller than the desired final dimensions to compensate.

Extrusion — the foundation

Extrusion is the most fundamental plastic process. Several others build on top of it. Master this and you've understood half the topic.

The principle: push softened plastic through a shaped opening (die). Whatever shape the die is, the plastic comes out in that shape — continuously. Like a play-doh extruder.

🍝
A pasta machine with a shaped disc on the end. Push pasta dough through, get spaghetti, fettuccine, or whatever shape the disc makes — continuously, in long strands.

Extruder anatomy

Hopper

Funnel where plastic pellets feed in.

Barrel

Heated cylindrical tube that holds the screw.

Screw

Rotates inside barrel — conveys pellets, melts them by friction, builds pressure toward the die.

Screen pack

Wire mesh filter — removes contaminants, builds pressure, straightens flow.

Die ⚠️

NOT part of the extruder! A separate, replaceable tool fitted at the end. Each die makes a different cross-section.

Mandrel

Pin in the centre of the die (held by spider legs) — creates hollow profiles like pipes and tubes.

$$Q_x = Q_d - Q_b$$

The screw pushes melt forward (Qd). The die resists, pushing some back (Qb). Net output is the difference.

Sheet and film production

Three different processes for making flat plastic. Don't confuse them.

Slit die extrusion

Polymer pushed through a wide letterbox-shaped die. Comes out as flat sheet. No mandrel, no air, no rolls.

Sheet (>0.5mm) and film (<0.5mm). Used for window glazing, packaging.

Blown film

Polymer extruded as a tube, inflated with air into a bubble that rises upward, cools at the frost line, then flattened by pinch rolls.

Carrier bags, cling film, bin liners.

Calendering

Polymer fed between progressively closer heated rollers — like a pasta machine. No extrusion at all, no die.

PVC shower curtains, floor tiles, pool liners.

Frost line — what controls film thickness

In blown film, the polymer solidifies at a specific height called the frost line. Higher frost line = more time for the bubble to stretch = thinner film. Lower frost line = less stretching = thicker film.

Injection moulding

Most widely used plastic process for discrete parts. Phone cases, pen barrels, plug sockets, LEGO bricks — all injection moulded.

Two units in the machine:

Injection unit

Melts and delivers plastic into the mould. Contains barrel, screw, and a non-return valve. Works like an extruder — but the screw also acts as a ram for injection.

Clamping unit

Holds two mould halves shut during injection — provides alignment and clamping force. ⚠️ Common exam trap to attribute this to the injection unit.

Shrinkage — five factors

The mould cavity is made oversized to compensate for plastic shrinking on cooling. Five factors affect how much it shrinks:

  • Higher injection pressure → reduces shrinkage (more material packed in)
  • Longer compaction time → reduces shrinkage (more time to pack material)
  • Higher temperature → reduces shrinkage (lower viscosity helps filling)
  • Fillers in plastic → reduce shrinkage
  • Thicker parts → INCREASE shrinkage (more material to contract)

Compression and transfer moulding

Compression moulding

Mainly TS. A measured charge placed in heated mould, pressed, cured, ejected. Like a waffle iron for thermosets.

Min 1,000 parts to be economical. Products: plug sockets, dinnerware, pot handles.

Transfer moulding

TS only. Charge placed in separate pot first, then forced through channels into mould cavity. Better flow into complex shapes.

Sits between injection and compression in production volume.

Blow moulding & rotational moulding

Blow moulding

TP only. Soft tube of plastic (parison) is inflated with air inside a closed mould — takes the mould's shape. Like glass blowing but with plastic.

Three variants: extrusion, injection, stretch blow moulding.

Products: bottles, containers, fuel tanks.

Rotational moulding

TP and TS. Powder placed inside hollow mould, rotated slowly in oven. Gravity coats the inside walls evenly.

Lower production rate. Large hollow parts.

Products: tanks, kayak hulls, playground equipment.

Thermoforming

TP ONLY. A flat plastic sheet is heated until soft, then pressed or vacuumed into a mould shape.

⚠️ Why TP only?

Thermoforming reheats a SOLID sheet. Thermosets are already cross-linked — they can't be softened by reheating. So thermoforming is fundamentally impossible for TS.

Two mould types:

  • Negative mould — concave cavity, sheet is sucked into the depression
  • Positive mould — convex shape, sheet is draped over the raised form

Products: blister packs (around tablets), yoghurt pot lids, bath tubs, fridge door liners.

Topic 3 — exam traps

Trap 1
"The die is part of the extruder."
FALSE. The die is a separate, replaceable tool — not part of the extruder itself. Each cross-section needs a different die.
Trap 2
"Thermosets can be thermoformed."
FALSE. Thermoforming requires reheating a solid sheet. Thermosets can't be softened by reheating once cured.
Trap 3
"The injection unit holds the two mould halves in alignment."
FALSE. The clamping unit holds the mould halves. The injection unit just melts and delivers the plastic.
Trap 4
"Higher polymer viscosity makes flow through small channels easier."
FALSE. Higher viscosity = harder flow. The whole point of heating polymers is to reduce viscosity for easier processing.
Trap 5
"Longer compaction time leads to greater shrinkage."
FALSE. Longer compaction time REDUCES shrinkage — more time to pack material into the cavity before it solidifies.
Trap 6
"Stretch injection blow moulding can process both thermoplastics and thermosets."
FALSE. All blow moulding is TP only. Thermosets can't be softened and inflated — they're cross-linked.
Trap 7
"Calendering offers poor surface finish and low gauge accuracy."
FALSE. Calendering is actually one of the best methods for surface finish and gauge accuracy. That's why it's used for premium products.

What makes an elastomer different?

A regular plastic stretches a bit and then breaks or deforms permanently. An elastomer stretches a LOT — sometimes 5-10× its original length — and snaps back. Why?

Three structural features:

  • Long flexible polymer chains — uncoil and recoil easily
  • Light cross-links between chains — stop them sliding past each other permanently, but allow flexing
  • Glass transition temperature (Tg) below room temperature — at room temp, chains are mobile and rubbery
Why does rubber spring back? — Entropy!

A randomly coiled chain has high entropy (lots of disorder). A stretched chain has low entropy (more ordered). Nature wants high entropy — so when you release the stretch, the chains spontaneously recoil. This is entropy-driven elasticity.

🍝
Imagine a bowl of cooked spaghetti with a few staples between strands at random points. The strands can flex and the staples allow movement — but they prevent the strands from sliding completely past each other. That's an elastomer. Heavy staples = thermoset. No staples = thermoplastic.

Key additives

Sulphur

The most important additive — creates cross-links between rubber chains during vulcanisation.

Carbon black

Reinforcing filler. Increases tensile strength + provides UV protection. That's why most rubber is black.

⚠️ The two-stage mixing rule

Sulphur is added LAST in mixing. If added at the start, mixing friction would heat the rubber, triggering premature vulcanisation in the mixer — destroying everything.

  • Stage 1 (masterbatch): All additives EXCEPT vulcanising agents
  • Stage 2: Add sulphur and accelerators after cooling

Vulcanisation — sulphur cross-linking

Discovered by Charles Goodyear in 1839. Sulphur atoms insert themselves between double bonds on neighbouring polymer chains, creating sulphur bridges that lock the chains together.

Original Goodyear process: 140°C for 5 hours. Modern process with accelerators: 15-20 minutes.

Low cross-link density

Soft, stretchy rubber. 1-10 cross-links per 100 carbon atoms.

Elastic bands, gloves, tyres, seals.

High cross-link density

Ebonite — hard rubber. Many more cross-links lock chains rigidly.

Bowling balls, hard protective cases.

Thermoplastic Elastomers (TPE)

A modern alternative to vulcanised rubber. Behaves like rubber in service but processes like a thermoplastic — meaning recyclable and no vulcanisation step needed.

Limitation

TPEs are not suitable for vehicle tyres — performance demands too high. They work fine for shoe soles, medical tubing, and even cycle tyres.

Polymer Matrix Composites (PMCs)

A composite combines two distinct materials to get properties better than either alone. PMCs are polymer + fibre. The polymer (matrix) binds everything together. The fibres (reinforcement) provide the strength.

🏗️
Like reinforced concrete — concrete is the matrix, steel rods are the reinforcement. Together they're way stronger than either alone. PMCs work the same way: polymer + fibres = composite.

Three main fibre types

Glass fibre

Cheap. Good stiffness, low cost. The everyday option.

Boats, wind turbine blades, car panels.

Carbon fibre

Strong & stiff. High strength-to-weight ratio. Expensive.

Aerospace, F1 cars, premium sports gear.

Aramid (Kevlar)

Tough. Best at absorbing impact energy.

Bulletproof vests, helmets, cut-resistant gloves.

PMCs are intentionally anisotropic

The fibres are aligned in the load direction. A composite is strongest along the fibres, weaker perpendicular. This is deliberate — engineering controls fibre orientation to resist expected loads.

Continuous vs chopped fibres

Continuous fibres

Run the full length of the part. Highest performance.

But: cannot flow into complex moulds. Must be placed deliberately.

Chopped fibres

Short random fibres. Moderate performance.

But: can flow into complex 3D mould shapes (used in injection moulding, compression moulding).

PMC manufacturing processes

Open mould — labour-intensive, low volume

Hand lay-up

Manual application of resin and fibre layers. Gel coat first, rolled to remove air, room temp cure.

Boat hulls, wind turbine blades, custom car bodies.

Spray-up

Resin and chopped fibres sprayed simultaneously. Faster but lower properties (random orientation).

Automated tape laying

Computer-controlled gantry lays prepreg tape. Aerospace wings.

Closed mould — chopped fibre systems

SMC — Sheet Moulding Compound

Sheet of resin with 20-50% chopped glass fibre. Used in compression moulding.

Higher fibre content = better structural performance.

BMC — Bulk Moulding Compound

Dough-like material with shorter fibres, lower content. Better flow → suitable for injection moulding and compression.

Continuous fibre processes — high performance

Filament winding

Continuous fibres pulled through resin bath, wound onto rotating mandrel in precise patterns. Hollow cylindrical parts.

Pressure vessels, LPG cylinders, pipes, rocket motor casings.

Pultrusion

Fibres pulled through resin bath then heated die. Constant cross-section profiles only.

Structural beams, ladder rails, bridge decking.

Why powder metallurgy?

If you can cast and machine almost anything, why bother with powder? Because some things genuinely can't be done any other way.

Powder production methods

Three main methods. Each gives different particle shape — and shape matters for how well the powder compacts.

Gas atomisation

Molten metal broken into droplets by high-velocity inert gas (Ar or N₂). Droplets solidify as spheres.

Spherical particles → excellent flowability → fills die easily → aerospace quality.

Expensive. Used for aerospace and medical powders.

Water atomisation

Molten metal broken by high-pressure water jets. Solidifies as irregular shapes.

Irregular particles → better interlocking → better compaction → cheaper.

Most common commercial method.

Electrolysis

Metal ions dissolved from an anode, deposited as powder at the cathode.

Very high purity powder. Used for copper, iron.

⚠️ MCQ trap: deposition is at CATHODE, not anode.

💧
Atomisation is like spraying water from a hose into cold air — the water breaks into droplets that solidify (freeze) mid-air. Gas atomisation uses inert gas instead of water, giving perfectly round droplets.

Blending vs mixing — MCQ trap

These sound the same but are defined differently in the module. Get them wrong and you lose marks.

Blending

Same composition, different particle sizes. You're combining Fe powder (fine) with Fe powder (coarse) to improve packing density.

No new alloy is created — just better particle size distribution.

Mixing

Different compositions. Fe powder + graphite powder = the start of steel. Cu powder + Sn powder = the start of bronze.

A new alloy composition is created during sintering.

Quick test

Iron powder + graphite powder — is that blending or mixing? Mixing, because they're different chemical compositions (Fe and C).

Sintering — how powder becomes metal

After pressing, the green compact looks like the right shape but it's just powder held together by mechanical interlocking. Sintering is what actually makes it a structural metal part.

Sintering temperature: 70–90% of the melting point (in Kelvin). Hot enough for atomic diffusion across particle boundaries. Not hot enough to melt.

🔥
Imagine ice cubes pressed together in a cold room. If you warm them slightly — not enough to melt — the surfaces begin to bond where they touch. The ice cubes fuse together at their contact points. That's exactly what sintering does to metal powder.

Secondary operations

Sizing

Light repressing after sintering. Corrects the 1–5% shrinkage. Improves dimensional accuracy and roundness of gear teeth.

Coining

Heavy repressing. Densifies surface regions. Improves surface finish and strength where contact occurs.

Infiltration

Lower-melting metal (e.g. bronze) placed on sintered iron part, heated, melts and wicks into pores by capillary action. Increases density and thermal conductivity.

Impregnation

Oil fills the pores. Creates self-lubricating bearings — oil seeps out during use, lubricates automatically, no maintenance needed.

HIP (Hot Isostatic Pressing)

After sintering — inert gas applies equal pressure from ALL directions at high temperature. Closes remaining pores. Near full theoretical density. Aerospace critical parts.

Heat treatment

Same methods as for solid steels — hardening, tempering, nitriding. Improves hardness, wear resistance, and fatigue life.

Memory device — secondary ops

Pressing (sizing, coining) → Filling (infiltration, impregnation) → Densifying (HIP) → Strengthening (heat treatment)

Advanced PM processes

MIM — Metal Injection Moulding

Powder + polymer binder mixed to make a feedstock, then injection moulded like plastic. Binder removed (debinding) then sintered. For small, complex, high-volume parts.

Surgical instruments, firearms components, dental brackets.

CIP — Cold Isostatic Pressing

Water applies equal pressure from all directions at room temperature. Uniform density without needing heat.

Spark sintering

Upper and lower punches act as electrodes — electrical discharge generates rapid localised heating at particle contact points. Much faster sintering than conventional.

Liquid phase sintering

One component melts during sintering, forming a liquid that fills gaps and promotes faster densification. Used for WC-Co (cemented carbide).

Plastic deformation — the fundamental concept

Before we look at any forming process, we need to understand what happens inside the metal when you force it into a new shape. This is what separates a forming engineer from someone who just "pushes metal around".

Metal forming relies on plastic deformation — permanent shape change that occurs when stress exceeds the yield strength. Unlike elastic deformation (which springs back), plastic deformation is permanent.

Elastic deformation

Below yield strength. Atoms displaced but bonds intact. Remove force → springs back completely. Like a spring.

Plastic deformation

Above yield strength. Atoms permanently rearranged via dislocation movement. Remove force → permanent change. Like bending a paperclip.

Work hardening — why metal gets harder as you form it

You've probably noticed that bending a paperclip repeatedly makes it stiffer and eventually it snaps. That's work hardening. Here's why it happens.

Metal crystals contain defects called dislocations — line defects in the crystal structure that allow slip (plastic deformation). When you plastically deform metal:

  1. Dislocations multiply dramatically
  2. Dislocations tangle and block each other's movement
  3. Further deformation requires more force
🚗
Imagine a traffic jam. One car moving is easy (low dislocation density). But as more cars fill the road, movement gets harder (high dislocation density). Work hardening is a traffic jam in the crystal structure.

Effects of work hardening:

  • Strength increases — harder to deform further
  • Ductility decreases — less ability to deform without fracturing

Recrystallisation — resetting the material

Heat the work-hardened metal to approximately 0.5 Tm (in Kelvin) and new strain-free grains nucleate and grow to replace the deformed ones. Dislocations eliminated. Strength drops back down. Ductility restored. Material reset.

Why recrystallisation matters for hot working

In hot working, recrystallisation occurs during deformation (temperature is above 0.5 Tm). So you can keep deforming without the material ever getting too work-hardened to continue. Like forming putty — it never stiffens up.

Bulk forming processes

Rolling — most widely used

Metal passed between rotating rolls that squeeze it, reducing thickness and increasing length. Like a pasta machine.

Flat rolling

Simple flat rolls → plates, sheets. Most common.

Shape rolling

Shaped groove rolls → I-beams, railway rails, channels.

Thread rolling

Cold rolling threads onto fasteners. Stronger than cut threads — grain flow preserved, surface work hardened.

Ring rolling

Thin wall ring made from thick ring. Hot for large, cold for small. Bearing races, pressure vessel rings.

Forging — compressed between dies

Open-die forging

Metal compressed between flat dies, flows freely outward. Simple shapes. Large parts. Like hitting clay with a flat board.

Impression-die forging

Shaped die cavity. Metal fills it. Flash is squeezed out at the die parting line — its resistance helps force metal into every corner. Crankshafts, connecting rods.

Flashless forging

Precision forging. No flash, no waste. Billet volume must exactly match die cavity. More precise but harder to set up.

Upset forging

Most common forging operation. Axial compression shortens and widens. Bolt heads, nail heads, rivets.

Extrusion — pushing through a die

Direct extrusion

Ram pushes billet toward fixed die. Metal flows in same direction as ram. Most common. Higher friction (billet slides against container).

Indirect extrusion

Die moves toward stationary billet. Metal flows backward through hollow ram. Lower friction — billet stays still.

Wire and bar drawing

Metal pulled through a die by tensile force — opposite of extrusion which pushes. Reduces cross-section. Usually cold worked, which work-hardens the wire increasing its strength. Used for electrical wire, spring wire, structural cables.

Sheet metalworking

Operations performed on thin sheet (0.4–6mm) using presses. Products called stampings.

"Blanking is used to create holes in sheet metal."
FALSE. Blanking produces the cut piece as the part (coins, discs). Holes are made by punching — where the slug is scrap.

"Thread rolling gives weaker threads than cutting because it deforms the material."
FALSE. Thread rolling gives stronger threads — grain flow follows thread contour, and surface is work hardened, improving fatigue resistance.

Why machine — and why not?

If machining gives such good accuracy and finish, why not machine everything? Because it has serious drawbacks that make other processes preferable for initial shaping.

✓ Advantages of machining
  • Excellent dimensional accuracy and surface finish
  • Works on virtually any solid material
  • Can produce complex geometries
  • Good for small batches
✗ Why not machine everything?
  • Material waste — removed material becomes chips, mostly wasted
  • Slow — not suited to high-volume production
  • Expensive — machining time + tool cost + setup
  • Often unnecessary — casting, forging, PM give near-net shape

Best practice: use casting/forging/PM to get close to shape, then machine only the critical features that need tight tolerances or good surface finish.

Three main machining operations

Turning

Workpiece rotates. Single-point tool moves linearly. Machine: lathe. Produces cylindrical surfaces.

Turning operations: facing, taper turning, contour turning, chamfering, cutoff, threading, boring.

Drilling

Tool rotates. Workpiece stationary. Machine: drill press. Creates round holes only.

Related ops: tapping (internal threads), countersinking (conical), counterboring (flat), reaming (sizing).

Milling

Tool rotates. Workpiece moves. Machine: milling machine. Multi-point cutter. Flat surfaces, slots, profiles.

Peripheral milling (axis parallel to surface) vs face milling (axis perpendicular).

⚠️ Critical distinction — turning vs milling

Turning: workpiece rotates. Tool is stationary (just moves linearly).

Milling: tool rotates. Workpiece moves into the rotating cutter.

This is consistently tested. Get it the wrong way round and you lose marks.

Boring vs turning — both on a lathe?

Turning = machining the outside diameter of a rotating workpiece.
Boring = machining the inside diameter of an existing hole — effectively internal turning.
Turning on a lathe: workpiece rotates. Boring on a drill press or milling machine: tool rotates.

Drilling detail — through holes vs blind holes

Through hole

Drill exits the opposite side. Clean exit. Simple.

Blind hole

Drill does NOT exit the opposite side. Flat or conical bottom. More care needed.

Tapping vs threading

  • Tapping — cuts internal threads into an existing hole (the nut side)
  • Threading — cuts external threads on a cylindrical surface (the bolt side)

Other machining operations

Broaching

Multi-tooth tool moved linearly in a single pass. Each tooth cuts slightly deeper than previous. Used for non-circular holes: keyways, square holes, internal splines, gear tooth profiles.

Shaping

Single-point tool moves back and forth across a stationary workpiece. Produces flat surfaces.

Planing

Workpiece moves back and forth past a stationary tool. Produces flat surfaces. Opposite motion to shaping.

Three ways a cutting tool fails

1. Fracture failure

Sudden tool breakage from high cutting force or interrupted cut. Unpredictable and dangerous — causes scrapped workpieces and can be violent.

2. Temperature failure

Excessive heat softens the cutting edge — loses its shape and cutting ability. Occurs when cutting speed is too high for the tool material.

3. Gradual wear ✓ preferred

Slow, progressive wear over time. This is what you want — predictable, gives maximum tool life, allows planned replacement before part quality degrades.

Tool wear — two locations

Crater wear

Depression worn into the rake face (top of tool) where the chip slides over it. The chip acts like sandpaper, grinding away material from above.

Like water carving a valley in rock — slow but persistent.

Flank wear

Flat worn area on the flank face (side of tool) where it rubs against the freshly machined surface.

Tool life criterion: replace tool at 0.5mm flank wear. Measured and monitored in practice.

Taylor tool life equation

Taylor tool life
$$v\, T^n = C$$
vcutting speed (m/min)
Ttool life (min)
nTaylor exponent (material + tool constant)
Cconstant (cutting speed for T = 1 min)

Key relationship: Higher cutting speed = shorter tool life. And it's non-linear — doubling speed reduces tool life by much more than half.

On a log-log graph, this becomes a straight line — that's how n and C are found from experimental data.

If you know v and need T: T = (C/v)^(1/n)

If you know T and need v: v = C / T^n

If you have two speed/life pairs to find n and C: take log of both sides and solve simultaneously — same as Hollomon in T6.

Tool materials — hardness ladder

Listed in increasing order of hardness and heat resistance:

High Speed Steel (HSS)

Maintains hardness up to ~600°C. Can be resharpened. Most versatile — drills, taps, reamers, broaches, milling cutters. Used at lower cutting speeds.

Cemented Carbides

Tungsten carbide (WC) with cobalt (Co) binder. Much harder and more heat resistant than HSS. Higher cutting speeds. Inserts common.

Coated Carbides

Cemented carbide with thin ceramic coating (TiC, TiN, Al₂O₃). Coating is 2.5–13 μm thick, applied by CVD or PVD. Even longer tool life.

Ceramics

Very high hardness and heat resistance. Brittle — not suitable for interrupted cuts or heavy feeds. High-speed finishing.

CBN (Cubic Boron Nitride)

Second hardest material. Excellent for hardened steels and superalloys. Doesn't react with iron.

Also remove chips, reduce thermal distortion, prolong tool life.

Primary job: reduce friction at tool-chip and tool-work interfaces. Best at low cutting speeds where friction is the dominant problem.

Drilling, tapping, reaming.

What makes grinding different?

A milling cutter has a precise number of cutting edges arranged at specific angles, cutting in a controlled way. A grinding wheel has millions of tiny abrasive grains, each with a random shape and negative rake angle — more friction, more heat, but capable of much finer material removal.

Conventional machiningGrinding
Cutting edgesDefined geometry, known rake angleRandom abrasive grains, mostly negative rake
SpeedLowerVery high (30+ m/s)
Material removalHighLow — precision removal
PurposeShape creationSurface finishing and precision
Heat generationModerateHigh — cooling critical
Grain action types

Not every grain actually cuts material:

  • Cutting — grain removes a chip (only a fraction of grains do this)
  • Ploughing — grain deforms the surface but doesn't remove material
  • Rubbing — grain slides without cutting — pure friction and heat

Grinding wheel anatomy

Three components — each has a critical job:

Abrasive grains

The cutting particles. Must be harder than the workpiece.

Materials: Aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) — general purpose. Silicon carbide (SiC) — harder materials. CBN and diamond — hardest applications.

Bond material

Holds grains together. Must be strong enough to hold grains during cutting but allow worn grains to release so fresh ones are exposed.

Vitrified (glass), resinoid, rubber, metal bonds.

Porosity

Spaces between grains. Essential for:

  • Chip escape during cutting
  • Coolant access to cutting zone
  • Preventing clogging

Wheel properties

Friability — the self-sharpening property

⚠️ Exam MCQ trap — the friability question

Friability = the ability of abrasive grains to fracture and expose new sharp edges.

Your exam paper may say "friability prevents grains from fracturing" — this is FALSE. Friability allows fracture. That fracture is what keeps the wheel self-sharpening.

  • Too little friability → grains stay dull → glazing (wheel surface becomes smooth and useless)
  • Too much friability → grains break away too fast → rapid wheel wear
✏️
Like a pencil that is designed to break in specific places as it wears — revealing a fresh sharp point. That's friability in grinding wheels.

Wheel grade vs wheel structure

Wheel grade = bond strength

How strongly the bond holds the grains.

  • Hard grade — grains held firmly, release slowly
  • Soft grade — grains released easily, continuously exposing fresh ones
Wheel structure = grain spacing

How far apart the grains are spaced.

  • Dense — more cutting edges, smoother finish, clogs faster
  • Open — more chip clearance, higher MRR, less clogging

Why hard material → soft wheel grade?

Hard material dulls grains faster. You need the bond to release dull grains quickly so fresh, sharp ones are exposed. A soft grade does this. A hard grade would hold dull grains in place — causing glazing.

Dressing vs truing

  • Dressing — sharpens the wheel by removing dull grains, exposing fresh sharp ones
  • Truing — reshapes the wheel to restore its geometry and concentricity

Ultra-precision finishing operations

After grinding, some applications need even finer finish. Three processes in order of increasing precision:

Honing

Abrasive sticks with combined rotational + oscillating motion. Creates characteristic cross-hatch pattern that retains lubricating oil.

Engine cylinder bores — the cross-hatch holds oil against piston rings.

Superfinishing

Very fine abrasive stone with light oscillating pressure. Removes peaks from previous grinding.

Bearings, precision shafts, instrument components.

Lapping

Abrasive slurry between two surfaces rubbing together. Highest accuracy of any finishing process.

Optical lenses, precision gauge surfaces, semiconductor wafers.

Precision order: Grinding → Honing → Superfinishing → Lapping

Trap 1
"Friability prevents abrasive grains from fracturing during grinding."
FALSE. Friability is specifically the property that ALLOWS fracture — this self-sharpening is desirable. "Prevents" is the opposite of what friability means.
Trap 2
"For hard workpiece materials, use a hard grade grinding wheel."
FALSE. Hard material → soft grade wheel. The wheel needs to release dull grains quickly when cutting hard materials.
Trap 3
"A dense wheel structure gives the highest material removal rate."
FALSE. Dense structure clogs with chips, reducing MRR. Open structure gives higher MRR because chips can escape freely.

Heat treatment — what and why

The same piece of steel can be made soft enough to be machined easily, or hard enough to cut through other metals — just by controlling how you heat and cool it. No chemistry changes. No new material. Just temperature and time.

Heat treatment causes both microstructural AND mechanical property changes. You're not just heating the metal — you're fundamentally changing the crystal structure inside, which changes how it behaves.

Key uses:

  • Soften metal for easier machining or forming (annealing)
  • Harden metal for better wear resistance (quench hardening)
  • Reduce brittleness after hardening (tempering)
  • Relieve internal stresses from forming or welding (stress-relief annealing)
  • Restore ductility after cold working (recrystallisation annealing)

The four heat treatment processes

Annealing

Heat to below melting point, soak, then slow cool (in furnace). Softens metal, improves ductility, relieves residual stresses, enables recrystallisation.

Analogy: letting glass-blown glass cool very slowly to relieve stress — annealing glass is the same principle.

Hardening

Heat steel above critical temperature (austenite phase), then rapidly quench. Produces martensite — very hard but brittle phase.

Always followed by tempering — hardened steel is too brittle to use.

Tempering

Heat previously hardened steel to intermediate temperature (below critical). Reduces brittleness while retaining most hardness.

⚠️ Tempering REDUCES brittleness — it does NOT increase hardness.

Quenching

The rapid cooling step in hardening. Different quench media give different cooling rates.

Fastest → slowest:

Brine → Agitated water → Still oil → Air

Steel phases — what forms on cooling

Why does quenching make steel hard? Why does slow cooling make it soft? The answer is in the crystal structure that forms depending on cooling rate.

Austenite

High-temperature FCC (face-centred cubic) phase. Dissolves carbon easily. Forms when steel is heated above the critical temperature (~723°C).

Martensite

Forms by rapid quenching of austenite. Carbon has no time to diffuse out — gets trapped in a distorted BCC structure. Extremely hard and brittle.

Hardness: very high. Ductility: very low.

Pearlite

Forms by slow cooling of austenite. Layered structure of ferrite and cementite. Much softer than martensite.

Hardness: low-medium. Ductility: reasonable.

⚠️ Pearlite vs Martensite hardness — common MCQ trap

Martensite is MUCH harder than pearlite. The exam may try to reverse this. Martensite = rapid quench = trapped carbon = distorted structure = HARD. Pearlite = slow cool = organised layered structure = SOFT.

TTT Diagram

Time-Temperature-Transformation diagram — a map showing which phase you get depending on how fast you cool the steel. Different cooling curves cross different transformation regions, giving different final microstructures.

TTT diagram — schematic. Ts = transformation start, Tf = transformation finish. Cooling rate determines which phase forms.

Precipitation hardening (age hardening)

A completely different mechanism from quench hardening — used for aluminium alloys, some stainless steels, and nickel superalloys. NOT available for all alloys.

1
Solution treatment
Heat to HIGH temp — all alloying elements dissolve
2
Quench
Rapid cool — supersaturated solution
3
Precipitation treatment
Heat to LOWER temp — fine precipitates form
⚠️ Solution treatment temp > precipitation treatment temp

Solution treatment is at higher temperature than precipitation (ageing) treatment. The exam may reverse this. Remember: you need high temp first to dissolve everything, then lower temp to let precipitates nucleate.

The fine precipitate particles block dislocation movement — this is what causes hardening. It's essentially the same mechanism as work hardening, but the obstacles are precipitates instead of other dislocations.

Nitriding = introducing nitrogen into steel surface at elevated temperature. Nitride precipitates form in the surface layer. The hardening mode is precipitation hardening — same principle.

Additive Manufacturing (AM)

Every process we've covered so far either removes material (machining) or reshapes it (forming, casting). AM is different — it adds material, layer by layer, from a digital CAD model. Complex internal channels, lattice structures, patient-specific implants — things impossible by any other method.

Also called: 3D printing, rapid prototyping, direct digital manufacturing.

Key AM processes

Powder Bed Fusion (PBF)

Thin layer of powder spread across build plate. Laser selectively melts regions. Platform lowers. Repeat.

Aerospace components, medical implants, complex internal channels. High quality, slow, expensive.

FDM — Fused Deposition Modelling

Heated filament extruded through nozzle, deposited layer-by-layer. Most accessible 3D printing process.

Prototypes, low-cost parts, concept models.

DED — Directed Energy Deposition

Powder or wire fed into a laser/electron beam melt pool. Used for large parts, repair of existing components, adding features.

AM advantages

  • Complex geometries impossible by traditional methods — internal channels, lattices
  • No tooling cost — geometry driven by CAD
  • Mass customisation — each part different at no extra cost
  • Reduced part count — complex assemblies made as single parts
  • Reduced waste — builds only what's needed

AM defects — four main types

Porosity

Most common. Gas entrapment, lack of fusion between layers, keyhole instability. Reduces strength and fatigue life.

Surface roughness

Layer-by-layer build creates staircase effect. Partially melted powder particles on surface. Usually requires post-processing.

Distortion

Thermal gradients during build cause residual stresses and warping. Support structures help manage this.

Powder degradation

Reused powder changes particle size distribution, satellite formation, composition variation — affects consistency.

AM design considerations
  • Columnar grains often observed — directional heat flow during layer melting
  • Build direction influences properties — anisotropic but NOT limited to vertical only
  • Support structures required for overhangs — NOT all AM parts are support-free
  • Warping can occur in some geometries
  • HIP often applied after AM to reduce porosity and improve fatigue performance

Semi-Solid Metal Processing (SSM)

Metal doesn't have to be fully liquid to flow. In the mushy zone — between liquidus and solidus — it's part solid, part liquid. With the right microstructure, you can control this state to get the benefits of both casting (complex shapes) and forging (better properties).

Key property: Thixotropy. At rest, semi-solid metal behaves like a solid. When sheared (stirred or pressed), it suddenly flows like a liquid.

🥣
Like ketchup — it sits solid in the bottle, but shake it and it flows. Semi-solid metal works the same way. Or like some yoghurts that become runny when stirred.

Why it works: Normal solidification creates dendritic (tree-branch) microstructure that clogs up and prevents flow. SSM requires a globular (spheroidal) microstructure — formed by shearing during solidification — which allows flow under stress.

Two processing routes

Rheo-route — from liquid

Start from liquid metal. Cool into mushy zone while shearing simultaneously. Shearing creates globular microstructure in one step. Semi-solid slurry injected directly into die.

  • ONE step — no intermediate solidification
  • Lower cost
  • Less control over microstructure
Thixo-route — from solid

Start from a specially pre-processed solid billet (already has globular microstructure). Reheat into mushy zone. Then inject into die.

  • TWO steps — solidification + reheating
  • Higher cost and complexity
  • Higher control and consistency
SSM advantages
  • Lower processing temperature than conventional die casting — less thermal stress on dies
  • Near-net shape — excellent dimensional tolerance
  • Lower porosity than conventional die casting
  • Better mechanical properties than casting, approaching wrought properties
  • Wide freezing range alloys preferable — gives larger processing window

Topic 10 — exam traps

Trap 1
"Tempering increases the hardness of hardened steel."
FALSE. Tempering REDUCES brittleness (and slightly reduces hardness). It does NOT increase hardness. Hardness was achieved by quenching to martensite.
Trap 2
"Pearlite has higher hardness than martensite."
FALSE. Martensite is much harder than pearlite. Martensite forms by rapid quenching (hard), pearlite by slow cooling (relatively soft).
Trap 3
"Solution treatment temperature is lower than precipitation treatment temperature."
FALSE. Solution treatment is at HIGHER temperature — all alloying elements must dissolve. Precipitation occurs at a lower intermediate temperature.
Trap 4
"All AM components are manufactured without support structures."
FALSE. Many AM components require support structures for overhanging features. These are removed after build.
Trap 5
"HIP improves surface roughness of AM parts."
FALSE. HIP improves internal porosity and mechanical properties. It does NOT improve surface roughness.
Trap 6
"Alloys with narrow freezing range are preferable for SSM."
FALSE. Wide freezing range alloys are preferable — they give a larger temperature window to work in, making the process more controllable.
Trap 7
"The thixo-route is a one-step process."
FALSE. Thixo-route is TWO steps — first solidification (to create billet with globular microstructure), then reheating into semi-solid state. Rheo-route is the one-step process.

Topic 2 — Casting

Casting T1
"Hot tearing is caused by flexible moulds restricting shrinkage."
FALSE. Caused by RIGID moulds restricting thermal contraction.
Casting T2
"In investment casting, the wax is destroyed and cannot be recovered."
FALSE. Wax CAN be recovered and reused — it melts out but is recyclable.
Casting T3
"Investment casting can produce very large parts."
FALSE. Investment casting IS size-limited — ceramic shell becomes too fragile at large sizes.
Casting T4
"Centrifugal casting can produce any shape."
FALSE. Only axisymmetric shapes — pipes, tubes, rings, wheels.
Casting T5
"Lost foam cannot have core shift defects because there is no core."
FALSE. Lost foam can still have core shift defects from foam pattern deformation.

"The die is part of the extruder."
FALSE. Die is a separate, replaceable tool — not part of the extruder machine.
Plastics T2
"Thermosets can be thermoformed."
FALSE. Thermoforming requires reheating a solid sheet — impossible for cured thermosets.
Plastics T3
"The injection unit holds the two mould halves in alignment."
FALSE. The clamping unit holds the mould halves.
Plastics T4
"Longer compaction time leads to greater shrinkage."
FALSE. Longer compaction time reduces shrinkage — more material is packed in.
Plastics T5
"Calendering offers poor surface finish and low gauge accuracy."
FALSE. Calendering gives excellent surface finish and high gauge accuracy.

"Positive mould has a concave cavity."
FALSE. Positive mould = convex shape. Negative mould = concave cavity.

"Carbon black provides no UV protection."
FALSE. Carbon black DOES provide UV protection — a major reason it's added.

"Sulphur is added at the start of mixing for best dispersion."
FALSE. Sulphur added LAST — adding early causes premature vulcanisation in the mixer.
