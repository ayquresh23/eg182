Topic 2 — Metal Casting

Sand casting mould anatomy

The gating system delivers molten metal from outside the mould to the cavity, while the riser provides a reservoir to compensate for shrinkage. Every component has a specific function.

Sand casting mould anatomy — cross-section view
Cope
Top half of the sand mould.
Drag
Bottom half of the sand mould. Sits on moulding board during preparation.
Flask
Rigid box (wood or metal) containing and supporting both mould halves.
Parting line
Interface where cope and drag meet. Leaves a visible seam on the finished casting.
Pouring basin
Funnel at top of mould. Receives molten metal smoothly, reducing turbulence and splash.
Sprue
Tapered vertical channel. Metal flows down. Tapered to prevent air ingestion as metal accelerates.
Runner
Horizontal channel at base of sprue. Distributes molten metal laterally to the gates.
Gate
Narrow entry point into mould cavity. Controls flow rate and is easy to snap off after solidification.
Cavity
The hollow space within the mould that defines the final shape of the casting.
Core
Sand insert placed in cavity to create hollow internal features. Also allows for shrinkage.
Chaplet
Metal support holding the core in correct position during pouring. Must have higher melting point than the cast metal.
Riser
Reservoir of molten metal that solidifies LAST. Feeds liquid metal into cavity to compensate solidification shrinkage.

Solidification grain zones

When metal solidifies in a mould, three distinct grain structure zones form from outside to centre, each with different mechanical properties.

Solidification grain zones in a casting — cross-section view showing chill, columnar, and equiaxed zones
Chill zone
Outermost layer at mould wall. Very fine, randomly oriented grains from extremely rapid cooling. Thin.
Columnar zone
Long grains growing inward against heat flow direction. Anisotropic — strong along grain direction, weak perpendicular.
Equiaxed zone
Centre grains — equal size in all directions. Isotropic. Best for general mechanical applications.
Isotropic
Properties same in all directions — equiaxed grains. Desirable for most engineering components.
Anisotropic
Properties differ with direction — columnar grains. Undesirable in castings, deliberate in composites.
Thermal arrest
Flat section on pure metal cooling curve. Temperature stays constant while latent heat of fusion is released.
Mushy zone
Semi-solid state between liquidus (TL) and solidus (TS). Part liquid, part solid. Prone to segregation.
Segregation
Non-uniform composition in alloy casting. Different elements freeze at different temperatures, causing concentration variations.

Topic 3 — Plastics Shaping

Extruder anatomy

The extruder is the workhorse of plastics processing. Understanding each component and its function is essential — and the die is not part of the extruder.

Single-screw extruder cross-section — showing all components and polymer flow direction
Hopper
Feed funnel at inlet of extruder. Holds plastic pellets and feeds them continuously into the barrel.
Barrel
Cylindrical heated tube containing the screw. Heats and pressurises polymer toward the die.
Screw
Rotates inside barrel — simultaneously conveys solid pellets forward, melts them by friction and conduction, and builds pressure for injection into the die.
Screen pack
Wire mesh filter positioned before the die. Removes contaminants, builds back-pressure, and straightens the rotating melt flow.
Die (extrusion)
Separate, replaceable shaping tool — NOT part of the extruder. Each cross-section profile needs its own die. Defines what shape the extrudate takes.
⚠️ Exam trap: commonly said to be part of the extruder — it is not.
Mandrel
Pin held in the centre of the die by spider legs. Creates the hollow centre in hollow profile extrusions such as pipes and tubes.
Die swell
Extrudate swells wider than the die opening as it exits. Polymer chains relax from their stretched, aligned state back to their natural coiled state.
Qx = Qd − Qb
Net output flow = drag flow (forward, from screw) minus back-pressure flow (reverse, from die resistance). The die resists flow, pushing some polymer back.

Polymer melt behaviour

Left: shear thinning behaviour vs Newtonian fluid. Right: die swell from viscoelastic memory
Viscosity
Resistance to flow. Higher molecular weight → higher viscosity. Higher temperature → lower viscosity. The measure of how "thick" the melt is.
Shear thinning
Viscosity decreases as shear rate increases. Polymer melts flow more easily the faster you push them. Non-Newtonian, pseudoplastic behaviour.
Like ketchup — shake hard and it suddenly flows.
Pseudoplastic
Technical term for shear-thinning behaviour. Viscosity depends on shear rate. Opposite of Newtonian (constant viscosity).
Viscoelasticity
Combined viscous (flow) and elastic (memory) behaviour in polymer melts. Chains are stretched during flow and want to return to coiled state.
Die swell
Extrudate swells wider than the die opening on exit. Viscoelastic chains relax from stretched, aligned state back to natural coiled state.
Frost line
In blown film extrusion: the height at which the rising bubble solidifies. Higher frost line = more stretching = thinner film. Controls final film thickness.

Topic 5 — Powder Metallurgy

PM process sequence

Complete PM process sequence — from raw powder to finished part
Green compact
Part after compaction — particles mechanically interlocked. Holds shape. Weak, porous, handleable. NOT yet sintered.
Like pressed chalk — holds shape but crumbles easily.
Sintering
Heating compacted powder to 70-90% of melting point (in Kelvin). Atomic diffusion creates bonds across particle boundaries. No melting occurs. 1-5% shrinkage results.
Neck formation
Stage 1 of sintering. Atoms diffuse across particle contact boundaries, forming small bonding bridges called necks. Fastest densification stage.
Pore shrinkage
Stage 2 of sintering. Necks grow larger, pores between particles reduce in size and become more rounded. Continued densification.
Grain growth
Stage 3 of sintering. Little further densification. Grain boundaries migrate and grains enlarge. Excessive grain growth reduces mechanical properties.
Blending vs Mixing
Blending = same composition + different particle sizes → improves packing. Mixing = different compositions → creates alloy precursors. Fe + graphite = MIXING.
⚠️ Exam trap: commonly confused.
Infiltration
Lower-melting metal (bronze) placed on sintered part, heated to melt, wicks into pores by capillary action. Increases density and thermal conductivity.
Impregnation
Oil forced into pores of sintered part. Creates self-lubricating bearings — rotation generates heat, oil seeps out and lubricates automatically. No maintenance needed.

Topic 6 — Metal Forming

Blanking vs Punching

These two operations both use a punch and die to cut sheet metal — but the desired product is different in each case.

Blanking vs Punching — the product distinction. Same tooling setup, different desired output.
Blanking
The cut piece IS the desired part. Surrounding stock is scrap. Used for coins, washers, discs, gaskets.
Memory: the "Blank" is what you keep.
Punching
The cut piece (slug) is the scrap. The surrounding sheet with the hole is the desired part. Used to create holes in brackets, panels, housings.
Springback
After bending, the elastic portion of the deformation recovers on releasing the force — the bend angle opens up. Fixed by overbending past the desired angle.
Deep drawing
Punch draws a flat blank into a die cavity, forming a hollow cup. Used for drinks cans, car body panels, kitchen sinks, ammunition shells.
Blankholder
Clamps the outer edges of the blank during drawing. Essential to prevent the flange from buckling under compressive stress (wrinkling defect).
Thread rolling
Cold rolling threads onto fasteners instead of cutting them. Stronger threads — grain flow preserved along thread profile, surface work-hardened. Better fatigue resistance.

Tool wear — crater and flank

Single-point cutting tool showing crater wear (rake face) and flank wear (flank face) locations

Grinding wheel structure

Grinding wheel cross-section showing the three components — grains, bond, and porosity
Abrasive grains
Hard cutting particles embedded in the wheel. Must be harder than the workpiece. Al₂O₃ (general), SiC (hard materials), CBN (steels), diamond (non-ferrous only).
Bond material
Holds abrasive grains together. Must be strong enough to hold grains during cutting but allow worn, dull grains to be released so fresh ones are exposed.
Porosity
Spaces between abrasive grains. Essential for: chip escape during grinding, coolant access to the cutting zone, preventing wheel clogging.
Friability
The ability of abrasive grains to FRACTURE and expose new sharp cutting edges. Causes self-sharpening behaviour. Too little → glazing. Too much → rapid wear.
⚠️ Exam trap: friability ALLOWS fracture, it does NOT prevent it.
Glazing
Grinding wheel surface becomes smooth and shiny. Dull grains remain because friability is too low. Wheel stops cutting, generates excessive heat, poor surface finish.
Wheel grade
How strongly the bond holds the grains. Hard grade = grains stay longer. Soft grade = grains released easily. Rule: HARD material → SOFT grade wheel.
Wheel structure
Spacing between abrasive grains. Dense = more cutting edges, better finish, clogs faster. Open = more chip clearance, higher MRR, less clogging.

Topic 4 — Rubber & PMC

Rubber processing sequence

Rubber processing sequence — compounding → mixing (two stages!) → shaping → vulcanising
Elastomer
Polymer with very large reversible elastic deformation. Three requirements: long flexible chains, light cross-links, glass transition temperature below room temperature.
Entropy-driven elasticity
Stretched polymer chains spontaneously recoil to their natural disordered state when released. Driven by thermodynamic tendency toward higher entropy (disorder).
Vulcanisation
Sulphur atoms form chemical bridges between polymer chains during heating. Creates permanent cross-links. Discovered by Charles Goodyear in 1839. Modern: 15-20 minutes.
Carbon black
Primary reinforcing filler. Increases tensile strength dramatically and provides UV protection. That's why most rubber is black.
⚠️ Exam trap: carbon black DOES provide UV protection.
China clay
Non-reinforcing filler (hydrous aluminium silicate). Cheaper bulk filler used when the product cannot be black in colour.
Cross-link density
Number of sulphur bridges between chains. Low density → soft rubber (elastic bands, tyres). High density → ebonite (hard rubber, bowling balls).

Topic 10 — Heat Treatment, AM & SSM

Heat treatment — quenching media and cooling rate

Quenching media cooling curves — brine (fastest) through to air (slowest). Rate determines final microstructure.
Annealing
Heat below melting point, soak, then SLOW cool (in furnace). Softens metal, improves ductility, relieves residual stresses. Reverses effects of cold working.
Hardening
Heat steel above critical temperature (~723°C) to form austenite, then rapidly quench. Produces martensite — very hard but brittle. Always followed by tempering.
Tempering
Heat previously hardened steel to intermediate temperature. REDUCES brittleness while retaining most hardness. Does NOT increase hardness.
⚠️ Exam trap: tempering reduces brittleness — it does NOT increase hardness.
Martensite
Forms by rapid quenching of austenite. Carbon has no time to diffuse — trapped in distorted crystal structure. Extremely hard and brittle. Highest hardness of any steel microstructure.
Pearlite
Forms by slow cooling of austenite. Layered structure of ferrite and cementite. Much SOFTER than martensite. Better ductility.
⚠️ Exam trap: martensite is harder than pearlite — not the other way round.
Precipitation hardening
Solution treatment (high temp) → quench → precipitation treatment (lower temp). Fine precipitate particles form that block dislocation movement → hardening. NOT available for all alloys.
Rheo-route (SSM)
Semi-solid processing from LIQUID. Cooled + sheared simultaneously into mushy zone. ONE step — no intermediate solidification. Lower cost, less control.
Thixo-route (SSM)
Semi-solid processing from SOLID billet. Pre-processed billet reheated into mushy zone. TWO steps — includes intermediate solidification. Higher control, higher cost.
